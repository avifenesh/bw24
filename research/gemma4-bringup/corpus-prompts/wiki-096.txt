--- title: at-least-once-delivery type: concept tags: [messaging, delivery-semantics, message-queue, distributed-systems, idempotency] sources: [] updated: 2026-06-29 --- # at-least-once-delivery At-least-once delivery is one of the three classic message-delivery guarantees a queue or streaming system can offer, alongside at-most-once and exactly-once. Under at-least-once, a message handed to the system is delivered one or more times: on a failure it is never lost, but it may be redelivered, so a consumer can see the same message twice.[^1][^2] At-most-once is the opposite trade — a message is delivered zero or one times, so it can be lost but never duplicated (fire-and-forget). Exactly-once is the ideal — each message takes effect once and only once even under partial failure — but it is expensive and frequently overclaimed: many systems advertising "exactly once" do not account for failures of a producer or consumer outside the system boundary.[^1] Kafka's own documentation is blunt that its default guarantee is at-least-once, with exactly-once reserved for transactional flows (idempotent producer + transactional offsets) added in version 0.11.0.0.[^1] The guarantee arises from the acknowledgement protocol.
