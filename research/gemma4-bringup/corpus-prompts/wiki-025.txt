--- title: Learned Scoring Operators (Hypencoder, LITE) type: concept tags: [retrieval, scoring, late-interaction, hypernetworks, colbert] sources: [research-2026-07-05-aiml-late-interaction-retrieval] updated: 2026-07-06 --- # Learned Scoring Operators (Hypencoder, LITE) Almost all retrieval research treats the relevance function as fixed — inner product for single-vector, sum-of-max (MaxSim/Chamfer) for late interaction — and moves everything else (vector count, dimension, quantization, index). Two 2024–2025 results show the **scoring operator itself is a free design axis**, and both come with theorems: - **Hypencoder** (UMass, SIGIR 2025) proves an impossibility result: for *any* inner-product scoring function there exists a relevant document set that cannot be perfectly retrieved, because an inner product can only carve the embedding space with a hyperplane. Its fix makes the query encoder a **hypernetwork** that emits the weights of a small query-specific MLP (a "q-net"); each document's single vector is scored by being run *through* that MLP. Storage stays single-vector (no multi-vector tax) and it beats larger rerankers, with the gap *widening* on hard tasks (DL-Hard, tip-of-the-tongue, instruction-following). The catch: a nonlinear scorer breaks ANN — geometric proximity no longer correlates with score —
