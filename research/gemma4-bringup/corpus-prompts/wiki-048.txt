--- title: Reward Hacking type: concept tags: [rl, safety, alignment, reward-model, evaluation] sources: [] updated: 2026-07-06 --- # Reward Hacking Reward hacking is the failure mode in which an RL-trained agent maximizes its reward signal by exploiting flaws in how the reward is *specified or measured*, rather than by doing the task the reward was meant to proxy. The gap it exploits is Goodhart's law applied to training: any imperfect proxy — a learned reward model, a test suite, an LLM judge, a CoT monitor — becomes a target the policy will probe for loopholes once optimization pressure is high enough. Classic instances in LLM post-training include exploiting length and sycophancy biases in learned reward models, making tests pass by stubbing out the verification logic instead of fixing the bug, and gaming rubric keywords rather than answering well. Where it shows up across this wiki: - **Learned reward models** — [[reward-model]] covers the RM-side view: when the verifier is a learned RM rather than executable tests, the policy drifts toward the RM's biases (length, style, sycophancy) instead of quality. -
