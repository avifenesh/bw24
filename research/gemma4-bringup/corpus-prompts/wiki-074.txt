Modern graph-based SLAM frames this as a **factor-graph optimization** problem rather than the older filtering (EKF/UKF) or particle-filter approaches. The key realization (circa 2006–2012) is that SLAM is fundamentally a **sparse nonlinear least-squares** problem that benefits from batch optimization over the whole trajectory ("smoothing") given good initial guesses — instead of maintaining a single covariance matrix like EKF-SLAM. Graph methods give full smoothing, natural handling of loop closures and revisits, sparse structure that incremental solvers exploit, decoupling of measurement models from the inference engine, and support for arbitrary sensors via custom factors. A **factor graph** is a bipartite graph of **variable nodes** — the unknowns to estimate, robot poses $x_i \in SE(3)$ and landmarks $l_j$ — and **factor nodes**, the probabilistic measurement constraints.
