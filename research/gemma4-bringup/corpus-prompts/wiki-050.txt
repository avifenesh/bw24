--- title: Centered Kernel Alignment type: concept tags: [representation-similarity, interpretability, distillation, metric, quantization] sources: [] updated: 2026-06-29 --- **Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)** is a similarity metric that quantifies how aligned two sets of neural representations are — typically the activations of two different layers, two different models, or a teacher and a student over the same batch of inputs. It was popularized for deep learning by Kornblith et al. in "Similarity of Neural Network Representations Revisited" (arXiv:1905.00414, 2019), building on the centered kernel alignment of Cortes et al. (2010/2012).[^1][^2] CKA's defining advantage over earlier measures such as CCA is that it can reliably match up corresponding representations in networks trained from different random initializations, which CCA cannot.[^1] Mechanically, CKA is built from Gram (kernel) matrices. Given two representation matrices `X` (n×p) and `Y` (n×q) whose rows are the same n samples, you form the Gram matrices `K = XXᵀ` and `L = YYᵀ`, center them with `H = Iₙ − (1/n)11ᵀ`, and compute the Hilbert–Schmidt Independence Criterion `HSIC(K,L) = tr(K_c L_c)/(n−1)²`.
