Core story: Cray didn't invent vector computing (Solomon, ILLIAC IV, CDC STAR-100, TI ASC all predate the Cray-1) — he diagnosed *why* STAR-100's memory-to-memory design underperformed (huge pipeline-startup cost + terrible scalar fallback, punished by Amdahl's Law) and fixed it with register-based vectors, chaining, and a runtime-variable vector-length register. That template ran supercomputing 1976–1995 and is explicitly revived today by RISC-V RVV and ARM SVE. ## Key takeaways - **Chaining is the single most important idea in the lineage**: a vector unit's *partial* output feeds the next unit's input clock-by-clock, no memory round-trip, no waiting for the full 64-element op. Without it the Cray-1 caps at 1 FLOP/clock; with 2–3 chains it sustained ~150 MFLOPS (peak 240) at just 80 MHz. Argonne 1978:
